\nA good photographer has to understand how the camera works. DSLR cameras offer a multitude of settings that can be intimidating if you are a beginner photographer.<\/p>\n

I hear this a lot:<\/p>\n

Professional photographers always shoot in full manual mode. Trust me: that is simply not true…<\/p>\n

…but pro photographers can shoot in full manual mode because they understand how all the settings of the camera influence the resulting photo.<\/p>\n

You know this already:<\/p>\n

Photography is all about light. Capture the right amount of light and your exposures will be correct.<\/p>\n

But lighting conditions are not always ideal. That is why you have to learn how to set your DSLR camera to compensate for the lack or excess of light.<\/p>\n

Capture too much light and your photo will be overexposed. Too little light and you get an underexposed image.<\/p>\n

Know this:<\/p>\n

A technically correct photo is not automatically a beautiful photo. But, it’s almost always a requirement for any good photo.<\/p>\n

This post is strictly about the technical side of taking pictures using a DSLR<\/strong>. So, if you are ready for the 3 secrets that will improve the quality of the pictures you take…<\/p>\n

…let’s begin!<\/p>\n

1. ISO Explained<\/h2>\n

The simple explanation:<\/p>\n

The ISO setting determines the sensitivity to light of your camera’s sensor. With numbers ranging from 100 up, a higher ISO setting means the sensor is more sensitive to light.<\/p>\n

What does that mean?<\/p>\n

It means that a higher ISO setting will allow taking pictures in lower lighting conditions. Higher ISO will brighten the image.<\/p>\n

But, there’s a trade off:<\/p>\n

Because the camera will be more sensitive, higher ISO settings will also increase the granular noise in the picture.<\/p>\n

Practical ISO Examples<\/h3>\n

Let’s see some examples of photos taken with different ISO settings.<\/p>\n

I took two pictures in full manual mode. First one is at ISO 100. As you can see the exposure is incorrect and I got an underexposed shot (the image is too dark).<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

By increasing the ISO setting to 800, leaving the other settings the same and keeping the same lighting conditions, I was able to get the correct exposure on the right.<\/p>\n

That looks better!<\/p>\n

So, by setting a higher ISO I managed to get the correct exposure. If you look at the detail on the bottom, there is some noticeable noise. However, sometimes you have to make a compromise and accept at least some noise in the picture if that allows you to get the right exposure.<\/p>\n

Let’s now see what happens if you abuse the ISO setting and you set it too high. For this I setup another example.<\/p>\n

I shot two other photos. One at ISO 100 and one at ISO 1600.<\/p>\n

This time I set the camera on a semi-automatic mode called P (Program Mode)<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

<\/p>\n

In this mode the DSLR camera will try to calculate the right exposure. I only changed the ISO and let the camera decide on the other settings. At ISO 1600 we are starting to see a lot of noise.<\/p>\n

The ISO setting is probably the easiest setting on your DSLR camera to understand.<\/p>\n

Keep this in mind:<\/h3>\n

In practice you will probably want to start by setting ISO 100 regardless of the camera mode settings. This will give you minimal noise.<\/p>\n

If the photo is underexposed or blurry, then keep all the other settings the same, increase the ISO and take another shot. Do this again until you get the right exposure.<\/p>\n

SECRET #1<\/h3>\n

How to Remove ISO Noise Using Photoshop<\/h2>\n

For removing ISO noise you will need to take several shots of the subject.<\/p>\n

Then open these shots in Photoshop using\u00a0File > Scripts > Load Files into Stack…<\/strong>\u00a0checking the\u00a0Create Smart Object after Loading Layers\u00a0<\/strong>checkbox<\/p>\n

This will create a smart object with the shots stacked inside it. With this smart object selected go to\u00a0Layer > Smart Objects > Stack Mode > Mean<\/strong>.<\/p>\n

Using this technique you will highly reduce or even remove most of the ISO noise grain.<\/p>\n

The more shots of the subject you stack, the better the result.<\/p>\n

2. Shutter Speed Explained<\/h2>\n

To take a picture, DSLR cameras quickly open and close a “small door” to let light reach the sensor. The “small door” is called shutter.<\/p>\n

Shutter speed determines how fast the shutter is opened and closed.<\/p>\n

More specifically:<\/p>\n

The shutter speed measures the amount of time that the camera sensor is exposed to light for taking a picture. The longer the time, the more light gets to the sensor and the image will be brighter.<\/p>\n

Also important:<\/p>\n

If your subject is moving you might get a blurry picture. Setting a faster shutter speed will allow you to get a sharper photo.<\/p>\n

SECRET #2<\/h3>\n

Avoid Camera Shake Using A Simple Rule<\/h2>\n

If you are not using a tripod you have to hold the camera very still when taking a shot. But even then, if the shutter speed is not fast enough you might get a blurry picture.<\/p>\n

How fast is fast enough for the shutter speed?<\/p>\n

I always use this rule:<\/strong> set the shutter speed to approximately 1\/x sec where x is equal to the focal length.<\/p>\n

For example: if I’m shooting at 50mm, a 1\/50 sec or faster will be enough for avoiding the camera shake. At 300mm you need to go even faster at 1\/300mm and so on.<\/p>\n

It’s all about the balance<\/strong><\/p>\n

You have to set a shutter speed fast enough to avoid blurry photos, but slow enough to get enough light to the sensor.<\/p>\n

Remember Secret #1 – ISO<\/strong><\/p>\n

The DSLR camera settings are meant to be used together.<\/p>\n

What if the shutter speed that gives you a good exposure is not fast enough to freeze the subject and it still looks blurry?<\/p>\n

The solution is:<\/p>\n

Increase the ISO. This will allow you to set faster shutter speeds and getting the same exposure levels. That is what I did in the example above to get the same level of exposure.<\/p>\n

To sum up:<\/p>\n

You can brighten an exposure using a slower shutter speed. And you can darken an exposure by setting a faster shutter speed. Also, you can eliminate blurry moving subjects with faster shutter speeds and increase the ISO to keep the correct exposure.<\/p>\n

All DSLR camera have a setting mode which allow you to set the shutter speed directly. For some cameras it’s marked as S (Shutter Priority)<\/strong> and on other cameras it’s marked as Tv (Time Value)<\/strong>.<\/p>\n
